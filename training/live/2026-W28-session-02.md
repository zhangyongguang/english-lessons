## New lesson evidence

The July 11 lesson confirms that target identities must remain unchanged. Agreement errors recurred with singular subjects (`our country don't`, `my country import`, `a person build`, `it help`). Plural and countability errors also recurred (`some breads`, `more business opportunity`, `many useful information`). Use these patterns to shape follow-ups, but do not reveal them before Jack answers.

## Teaching protocol

1. Ask one question at a time and let Jack finish.
2. Do not announce which target a question is testing.
3. Do not interrupt answers. Give feedback after each complete answer.
4. For a target error, ask Jack to locate and repair it without giving the corrected sentence.
5. Give a specific hint after one failed attempt and the answer only after two failed attempts.
6. Ask for the complete sentence or thought again after correction.
7. Count prompted repairs separately from correct first attempts.
8. Ignore unrelated minor errors unless meaning breaks down.

## Round 1 — Unprompted habits

Ask these one at a time, with natural follow-ups:

- What does a reliable coworker do when a production service fails?
- How does your manager normally respond when someone reports a problem?
- What does a good teacher do when a student doesn't understand a question?

Create at least 10 natural opportunities for present-tense third-person verbs, including affirmative forms and `doesn't + base verb`. Do not ask Jack to state the rule.

## Round 2 — Past narrative retrieval

Ask:

> Tell me about the last technical problem you solved. Start with how you discovered it and finish with what happened afterward.

Follow up with:

- Who noticed the problem first?
- Which steps did you take?
- Which services or machines were affected?

Do not remind Jack to use the past tense or plurals.

## Round 3 — Distractor topic

Talk for about five minutes about food, commuting, or weekend plans. Continue recording target opportunities without announcing a test.

## Round 4 — Delayed and transferred retrieval

Ask:

> Imagine a hospital receives complaints from several patients. What does the hospital normally do, and what did it do after the most recent complaint?

Use follow-ups involving doctors, nurses, procedures, reports, and responsibilities. This answer should naturally require all three targets.

## Round 5 — Final assessment

Ask:

> Describe one person in your company: what that person usually does, what they did last week, and which projects or services they work on.

Do not coach during the answer. Give feedback only after it is complete.
