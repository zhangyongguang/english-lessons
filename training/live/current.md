## Targets

### 1. Third-person subject–verb agreement

- Trigger: a present habit with `he`, `she`, `it`, one person, one company, or one organization.
- Common pattern: “The manager need...” → “The manager needs...”
- Pass condition: at least 8 correct first attempts out of 10, one delayed retest, and two transfer contexts.

### 2. Past tense in completed-event narratives

- Trigger: yesterday, an earlier stage of life, a completed activity, or completed research.
- Common pattern: “I have a good day today.” → “I had a good day today.”
- Pass condition: at least 8 correct first attempts out of 10, one delayed retest, and two transfer contexts.

### 3. Plural marking on countable nouns

- Trigger: two or more countable people, objects, places, events, services, or responsibilities.
- Common pattern: “some home shopping channel” → “some home-shopping channels”
- Pass condition: at least 8 correct first attempts out of 10, one delayed retest, and two transfer contexts.

The July 14–17 lessons provide fresh evidence for all three targets: singular subjects still lost agreement (“a friend who is firefighter” → “friends who are firefighters”), completed events shifted into present tense (“I didn't think it looks...” → “it looked”), and plural marking failed after `some` and in general group references (“in some emergency situation” → “in emergencies”). Use unseen subjects, nouns, and completed events rather than quoting the corrected examples before Jack answers.

## Teaching protocol

1. Ask one question and let Jack finish his answer.
2. Track only the three targets; ignore unrelated minor errors unless meaning breaks down.
3. If a target error occurs, name the target but do not give the corrected sentence.
4. Ask Jack to self-correct. Give a specific hint after one failed attempt and the answer only after two failed attempts.
5. Ask Jack to repeat the complete answer, not only the corrected word.
6. Count prompted repairs separately from correct first attempts.
7. Continue with another topic for about five minutes, then retest each form without announcing it.
8. Retest the forms again in a different context.

## Past narrative and plural follow-ups

Ask:

> Tell me about the last busy day you had, from the first thing you did until the end of the day.

Ask one follow-up at a time about the people, meetings, messages, tasks, places, products, and problems involved. Create at least ten natural plural opportunities and enough completed actions for ten past-tense opportunities.

## Delayed retrieval

After about five minutes on a neutral distractor such as food or weather, ask without announcing a test:

> Think about the last family activity you attended. What did each parent do, and which games, objects, and places were involved?

## Transfer

Ask:

> A hospital receives complaints from several patients. What does it normally do, and what did it do after the most recent complaint?

Use follow-ups about one doctor, one manager, several services, several rooms, and the steps completed yesterday.

## Final production

Do not coach during this task:

> For one minute, describe one person in your company: what that person usually does, what they did last week, and which projects, customers, services, or tools were involved.
